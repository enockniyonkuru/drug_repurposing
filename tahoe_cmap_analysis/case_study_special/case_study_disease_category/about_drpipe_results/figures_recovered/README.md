## Results for Manuscript

### Comparative Analysis of CMAP and Tahoe Signature Databases for Computational Drug Repurposing

To evaluate the utility of gene expression signature databases for drug repurposing, we compared the recovery of known drug-disease associations using two distinct perturbation resources: the Connectivity Map (CMAP), derived from diverse cell lines treated with bioactive compounds, and the Tahoe database, generated from cancer cell line perturbations. Our signature-reversal pipeline identified 948 known drug-disease pairs using CMAP signatures (113 unique diseases, 240 unique drugs) and 2,198 pairs using Tahoe signatures (148 unique diseases, 196 unique drugs), representing a 2.3-fold increase in recovery rate for Tahoe. Notably, only 160 pairs (5.4% of the combined set) were recovered by both databases, while 788 pairs were uniquely identified by CMAP and 2,038 by Tahoe, suggesting these resources capture complementary biological information rather than redundant signals.

Analysis of drug target class distributions revealed striking mechanistic differences between the two databases. Tahoe-recovered drugs were predominantly enzyme inhibitors (52.3% of expanded combinations), consistent with the prevalence of kinase inhibitors and metabolic enzyme modulators in oncology drug development. In contrast, CMAP showed a more balanced target class distribution, with enzymes (29.8%), transcription factors (24.1%), membrane receptors (15.3%), and ion channels (10.7%) all substantially represented. This broader mechanistic coverage likely reflects CMAP's more diverse cell line panel and compound library, which extends beyond oncology-focused therapeutics.

The therapeutic area analysis further illuminated the biological basis for these differences. While both databases showed enrichment for oncology-related diseases, Tahoe demonstrated a pronounced cancer bias, with 31.0% of expanded combinations involving Cancer/Tumor therapeutic areas compared to 20.9% for CMAP. Tahoe also showed stronger recovery for reproductive/breast diseases (11.5% vs. 7.9%), potentially reflecting the breast cancer cell line representation in the Tahoe dataset. CMAP, by contrast, showed relatively stronger performance in nervous system and cardiovascular disease areas, consistent with its broader compound library that includes CNS-active agents targeting membrane receptors and ion channels.

Network analysis of disease-drug target relationships revealed distinct connectivity patterns. In Tahoe, the strongest connections linked oncology therapeutic areas to enzyme-targeting drugs, forming a dominant hub in the disease-drug target network. CMAP exhibited a more distributed topology with multiple moderate-strength connections across diverse therapeutic areas and drug target classes. Radar plot comparisons across eight common therapeutic areas confirmed that even for the same disease category, CMAP and Tahoe recovered drugs with different mechanistic profiles, suggesting that the choice of signature database should be guided by the therapeutic area and drug target class of interest.

These findings demonstrate that CMAP and Tahoe serve complementary roles in computational drug repurposing. Tahoe's cancer cell line foundation makes it particularly powerful for identifying enzyme-targeting repurposing candidates in oncology and related areas, while CMAP's broader biological coverage provides superior performance for non-oncology applications and receptor-targeted therapeutics. For comprehensive drug repurposing screens, we recommend integrating predictions from both databases to maximize coverage across therapeutic areas and drug mechanisms, as their union recovered 2,986 unique drug-disease pairs—representing 3.1× more candidates than either database alone.

## Color Convention

Throughout all visualizations, a consistent color scheme is used:

### Pipeline Colors (for comparison figures)
- **CMAP**: Warm Orange (`#F39C12`) 🟠
- **Tahoe**: Serene Blue (`#5DADE2`) 🔵

### Special Color Schemes

**Bubble Charts** - Use intensity colormaps to show relationship strength:
- CMAP: `YlOrRd` (Yellow-Orange-Red gradient) - lighter = fewer pairs, darker = more pairs
- Tahoe: `YlGnBu` (Yellow-Green-Blue gradient) - lighter = fewer pairs, darker = more pairs

**Chord/Network Diagrams** - Use distinct colors for node types:
- Disease Therapeutic Areas: Red/Coral (`#E74C3C`) 🔴
- Drug Target Classes: Green (`#27AE60`) 🟢

## Technical Notes

1. **Multi-membership expansion**: Diseases and drugs can belong to multiple categories (therapeutic areas / target classes). The "expanded combinations" count reflects all possible category combinations for each recovered pair.

2. **Normalization**: Comparative heatmaps and stacked bars use row-wise percentage normalization to enable fair comparison between pipelines with different total pair counts.

3. **Top-N filtering**: For readability, most visualizations show only the top N categories (typically 8-15). Full data is available in the source CSV files.

4. **Color consistency**: All figures follow the CMAP=Orange, Tahoe=Blue convention to enable intuitive cross-figure comparison.
