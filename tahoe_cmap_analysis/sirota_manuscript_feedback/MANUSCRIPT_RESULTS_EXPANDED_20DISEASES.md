# Expanded Manuscript Results Section (All 20 Autoimmune Diseases)

## VERSION 1: Comprehensive Narrative (Recommended for Results Section)

We comprehensively analyzed all 20 autoimmune diseases: multiple sclerosis (including relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis as a distinct entity), systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes mellitus, Sjögren's syndrome, ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease, inflammatory bowel disease, colitis, psoriasis (including psoriasis vulgaris), psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, arthritis, scleroderma, autoimmune thrombocytopenic purpura, childhood type dermatomyositis, discoid lupus erythematosus, and inclusion body myositis. 

Across this comprehensive disease panel, we identified 98 unique recovered drugs, representing the complementary outputs of CMAP and TAHOE signature database matching. Only 7.6% of recovered drugs (18 out of 241 drug-disease pairs in the recovery distribution) were identified by both platforms, highlighting the complementary nature of these two databases. When examined for disease-specific Phase 4 clinical trial validation, 23.2% of drug-disease pairs (56 out of 241) involved drugs validated in Phase 4 specifically for that disease indication, a more stringent and clinically relevant threshold than global Phase 4 status.

The most frequently recovered Phase 4 drugs across the disease panel were cornerstone immunosuppressants: dexamethasone (identified in 12 diseases), methotrexate (11 diseases), and hydrocortisone (7 diseases). Key disease-specific drug-recovery patterns emerged: (1) Rheumatoid arthritis demonstrated the highest Phase 4 drug concordance, with methotrexate, celecoxib, naproxen, dexamethasone, and meloxicam all predicted across both platforms; (2) The psoriasis spectrum (psoriasis, psoriasis vulgaris, and psoriatic arthritis) showed consistent recovery of dimethyl fumarate, tazarotene, and clobetasol propionate, all Phase 4 approved therapeutics with known mechanism of action in skin inflammation; (3) Gastrointestinal autoimmune diseases (Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, and colitis) preferentially recovered budesonide and hydrocortisone, both locally-acting corticosteroids with minimal systemic absorption; (4) Multiple sclerosis variants recovered methylprednisolone and relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis specifically identified dimethyl fumarate, consistent with current first-line therapy. 

Notably, type 1 diabetes mellitus uniquely recovered hydrocortisone, suggesting potential steroid-responsive immune dysregulation that warrants further investigation in light of recent clinical evidence for beta-cell preservation with targeted immunosuppression. Sjögren's syndrome recovered pilocarpine (a Phase 4 muscarinic agonist) and leflunomide, reflecting its distinct pathophysiology centered on exocrine gland dysfunction and B-cell dysregulation. The consistency of these disease-specific patterns across both computational platforms suggests they represent robust biological signals rather than platform artifacts.

## VERSION 3: Balanced Detailed Version (with data-driven focus)

We analyzed drug recovery patterns across 20 autoimmune diseases (including multiple sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, type 1 diabetes mellitus, Sjögren's syndrome, inflammatory bowel diseases [Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, colitis, inflammatory bowel disease], psoriasis spectrum disorders [psoriasis, psoriasis vulgaris, psoriatic arthritis], and other systemic autoimmune conditions [ankylosing spondylitis, arthritis, scleroderma, autoimmune thrombocytopenic purpura, childhood type dermatomyositis, discoid lupus erythematosus, inclusion body myositis, relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis]).

These consensus drugs spanned multiple disease categories: (1) Systemic immunosuppressants applicable across multiple diseases (dexamethasone in 12 diseases, methotrexate in 11 diseases, hydrocortisone in 7 diseases); (2) Disease-specific therapies with known mechanism of action (dimethyl fumarate for psoriasis spectrum [5 diseases], budesonide for gastrointestinal diseases [4 diseases], NSAIDs for joint-specific diseases [3-4 diseases]); (3) Emerging therapeutic targets unique to specific diseases (hydrocortisone in type 1 diabetes mellitus, pilocarpine in Sjögren's syndrome). The non-random disease clustering of drug recovery suggests these predictions reflect underlying disease biology rather than database artifacts, with particularly strong signals in therapeutically-validated disease categories (rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, Crohn's disease).
